For this project I redesigned the Department of Education's landing page and FAFSA form. This started as a group project, which then transistioned into an independant one. While we were in our group I assumed a leadership position and spearheaded ideas, coordinated our meetings, and made sure all team members had their deliverables done on time.

We conducted the user research through in person interviews and over zoom. We interviewed 8 people in total and put our put our findings in a miro board and compared their similarities and differences. Unsurprisingly, we found that our users were frusterated with the overall architecture of the Department of Education on both the desktop and mobile. They felt they needed more guidance from the navigation, and a more intuative site map.

For our user persona we created Cecelia Davidson, who is a 40 year old single mom who is looking for scholorships for her 17 year old son so she can afford to pay for his college tuition. We wanted to create someone who was making less than 60K a year, and who may not have gone to college themselves.

My team and I established a user flow and then conducted heuristic evaluations on the site's navigation to see what was actually accessible for the user and what wasn't. We found that most of the information on the site was presented in through clickable links instead in actual copy, and that each of these links lead to different pages that ultimately had very little information yet still had many links.

We created a user flow that followed the navigation from the landing screen to successfulling completeing the FAFSA form.

From there we split into individual teams and I worked on card sorting. I initially had the Contact, Help, Press, Language, and Covid pages on the top navigation bar, but in the high-fidelity they ended up on the footer, as they weren’t as crucial.

As I created the low-fidelity prototype, I wanted to create a footer and header that clearly labeled the different pages of the site. I wasn’t sure how to organize it, so I gave it a simplistic set up that would be easy to work with in later iterations.

Working on the mid-fidelity wireframe, I wanted to keep the blue that was used in the intial design, but I also created copy that was supportive and direct to the viewer. I had similar ideas when chosing Questrial as the font and my images, as they were both formal, engaging, and could appeal to a wider age demograpic.

My high-fidelity wireframe was similar to my mid-fidelity, but with a full drop down menu, highlighted icons, and a blue text field under the copy that was on the images. I added the search bar to the top header as this was easier for the user to engage with, and kept the login as a separate part of the navigation.

I conducted seven user tests, and found that a majority of them found the pages in the mobile seemed to blend together as the copy was similar on each page. With the desktop, I found that my users were confused by the button "Back", which would enable the user to return to the home screen. For the mobile, I added icons on the pages to reflect what task had been achieved on that page, and for the desktop I changed the button to "Return".

In the end I learned a lot from this assignment. My foremost concern was to design a userflow that reflected the support and ease that was clearly missing in the original flow. I felt that though my designs are more engaging that the current DoED, I believe I still should add more icons to the desktop, and should add a process bar to the FAFSA pages.
